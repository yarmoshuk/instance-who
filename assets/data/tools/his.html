1 Introduction

Health Information Systems

A standardised system to monitor refugee public health and HIV programmes in camp and urban settings

How does it work?

1.Collection

Data is collected in health facilities in camp or urban settings.

2.Data Entry

Data is entered into an Excel sheet.

3.Reporting

Data is submitted from the Excel sheets directly to:

  • – Twine (for urban HIS);or
  • – HIS database (for camp HIS).
4.Analysis

HIS data is automatically analysed and converted into indicators.

5.Review

Results are reviewed and approved at Regional and Headquarters level.

6.Action

Final indicators are visualized and shared with partners to inform public health action.

What is it?

HIS is a standardised system to monitor refugee public health and HIV programmes in camp and urban settings.

What are the objectives?

The objectives of the Health Information System (HIS) are:
  • To rapidly detect and respond to public health problems and epidemics
  • To monitor trends in public health status and continually address health-care priorities
  • To evaluate the effectiveness of interventions and service coverage
  • To ensure that resources are correctly targeted to areas and groups of greatest need
  • To evaluate the quality of health programmes.

Is it appropriate for me?

The Health Information System can be classified as follows:
Setting
 Appropriate for camp and urban settings
Operational Phase
 Appropriate for emergency and protracted settings
Income Setting
 Appropriate for low and middle/high income settings
Method
 Monitoring tool
Sector
 Collects Public Health,RH/HIV/AIDS and nutrition data
Technology
 Entered into an Excel sheet
Unit of Measurement
 Measures indicators at the health facility level

What does it collect?

Data is collected within the following sections in the HIS:
  • Population*
  • Mortality
  • Morbidity
  • Inpatient department and Referral
  • Laboratory
  • Disease Control
  • EPI and Vitamin A
  • Nutrition
  • Reproductive Health
  • HIV/AIDS.

All sections are optional and can be configured according to the services that are offered in the health facility by the specific partner.

*Note the population section should only be turned on in exceptional cases in urban settings (see guidance notes).

Why is it important?
  • Mortality rates are key indicators of the severity of a public health emergency.They are also core indicators for UNHCR's Global Strategic Priorities (GSPs).

Who should use it?
  • HIS is designed to be used by health partners working in both camp and urban settings.

What are the key differences between camp and urban HIS?

There are two versions of the HIS:one for camps and one for urban settings.
The urban HIS has 3 key differences when compared to the version used in camps:
  • It can monitor a"dual burden"of communicable and non-communicable disease.
  • It has an expanded section to monitor referrals to secondary and tertiary care.
  • It does not rely on population denominators.

How long will it take?
  • The time needed to compile an HIS report depends on the capacity and number of staff that are available.Normally,a weekly HIS report should take no more than 3 hours to complete.

How often do I need to do it?
  • HIS reports can be submitted on a weekly or a monthly basis.Weekly reporting is recommended,as this is more suitable for disease surveillance and is also reduces errors as results are automatically aggregated at the end of each month

What are the limitations?
  • Both the camp and urban HIS rely on facility-based reporting of health events.Therefore,it is vital to build strong linkages with community-health programmes to ensure good coverage of any health events that occur in the community.If this is not the case,then HIS will under-report important events that take place in the community (such as births and deaths).
  • The urban HIS is only intended for use in health facilities that are managed by UNHCR implementing partners.Data obtained from the urban HIS will therefore not be representative of the entire population of concern in urban settings.For this reason,the results from the urban HIS should be triangulated with those from other urban tools (such as Balanced Scorecard and Prospective Surveillance) to obtain a comprehensive understanding of urban public health needs.

What other tools are relevant?
  • The camp HIS is designed to be used alongside the majority of other tools available in Twine.The key tools include the Balanced Scorecard,WASH report card,Outbreak Reporting System,Maternal Death Reviews and Nutrition Survey database.
  • The urban HIS is designed to be interpreted alongside the Balanced Scorecard and the Prospective Surveillance tools.

Who will review the reports?
  • After the HIS reports have been submitted to Twine they will be reviewed and approved at country level by the UNHCR health coordinator.
  • Next,they will be reviewed at Regional level.
  • No Headquarters approval is needed for routine HIS reports.
  • The final HIS reports will be made publicly available after they have been approved at all other levels.

How is the quality of data assured?

The quality of data in the HIS is upheld in the following ways:
  • The Excel sheets where data is first entered have validation rules to only accept appropriate values.
  • The indicators are automatically aggregated in a database,to reduce the possibility of human error.
  • Each HIS report is reviewed at country and regional level to ensure results are carefully examined and corrected where necessary.
  • A data quality algorithm is automatically run on each report,and feedback provided to countries on the timelines,completeness and quality of data in their HIS.

Where can I get help?
  • For more help and support,please contact HQHIS@unhcr.org
